## TOPICAL REVIEW

## Harms associated with retinal implantation of a stimulating electrode array to treat outer-retinal degeneration: a systematic review and meta-analysis of safety

Luke E Hallum1\\* and Shaun L Cloherty

1 Faculty of Engineering, University of Auckland, Auckland, New Zealand  2 School of Engineering, RMIT University, Melbourne, Australia  \* Author to whom any correspondence should be addressed.

E- mail: L.hallum@auckland.ac.nz and shaun.cloherty@rmit.edu.au

Keywords: retinal implant, systematic review, safety, vision prosthesis, meta- analysis, adverse events, retinitis pigmentosa

## Abstract

Retinal implantation of an electrode array is an emerging treatment for vision loss caused by outer- retinal degeneration. This article collects and analyses harms associated with the treatment reported in the peer- reviewed literature, thus enabling informed decision- making by patients, clinicians, researchers, engineers, and policymakers. We searched MEDLINE, Embase, and clinical trials registries for peer- reviewed journal articles reporting harms outcomes. We extracted data from articles including study design, definitions of 'serious adverse event', and timing of adverse events. We applied the McMaster tool to these articles to assess the risk of bias in harms assessment and reporting. Our searches returned 585 abstracts. We reviewed the full text of 59 articles describing 11 different devices. McMaster scores ranged from 3 to 12 (maximum 15; higher scores indicate less risk). We compiled a comprehensive list of all serious and non- serious adverse events associated with retinal implantation. Several harms were common across devices. Our meta- analysis showed that serious adverse events are log- uniformly distributed throughout follow- up. Improved reporting and further clinical studies are needed to develop a reliable safety profile of retinal implantation. Our findings will help guide the design, conduct, and reporting of future clinical trials of retinal implantation and other emerging treatments for vision loss. (PROSPERO registration: CRD42022308123. )

## 1. Introduction

Retinitis pigmentosa (RP) and age- related macular degeneration (AMD) are two common outer- retinal diseases that can result in permanent and total blindness [1- 3]. RP affects one in 4000 [4, 5]. In the United States, late- stage AMD affects one in 100 people over 40 years old [6]; the global prevalence of early- and late- stage AMD is approximately \(8\%\) [7]. RP, AMD and similar outer- retinal diseases are characterised by photoreceptor loss, but a tendency to spare inner- layer neurons such as retinal ganglion cells [8].

Retinal implantation of a stimulating electrode array is an emerging treatment for outer- retinal diseases. The implanted array replaces photoreceptor function by electrically activating viable inner- retina (reviewed by Weiland and colleagues [9]). These vision prostheses typically comprise an electrode array surgically implanted either epiretinally, subretinally, or suprachoroidally. The array is typically coupled to an imaging light sensor device (e.g. a head- mounted CCD camera) through a wired or wireless interface. More than 500 patients have received retinal implantation, and, worldwide, approximately 40 groups are working on the development of retinal implants [10].

This is the first systematic review and meta- analysis of safety of retinal implantation of an electrode array to treat outer- retinal degeneration, and a companion paper to our previous systematic review of device efficacy [11]. Indeed, much attention has been paid to the efficacy of retinal implantation [11, 12]. However, understanding both the efficacy and safety of retinal implantation is clearly important. The purpose of this

review is to collect and analyze harms reported in the peer- reviewed literature. This collection and analysis is intended to be used to better inform decision- making by (1) patients and clinicians, (2) clinical researchers designing clinical studies, (3) biomedical engineers designing devices, and (4) regulators, institutional review board (IRB) panellists, and others responsible for weighing the risks and benefits of implantation.

## 2. Methodology

### 2.1. Data sources and study selection

We searched MEDLINE (Ovid) for entries labelled with the MeSH Heading 'Visual Prosthesis' (date of last search: 6 December, 2023). We filtered the results of that search using a filter comprising generic adverse events terms which pertain to medical devices [13], as well as specific adverse events terms either known to be associated, or plausibly associated, with visual prosthesis (e.g. 'subretinal haemorrhage'). The combination of generic and specific adverse events terms has been shown to increase search sensitivity [13]. Additionally, we searched MEDLINE for titles or abstracts containing synonyms for 'visual prosthesis' (e.g. 'retinal implant' or 'subretinal prosthesis'; appendix A). We also filtered the results of that search using the above- described filter combining generic and specific adverse events terms. We limited all search results to journal articles published in English describing studies of human participants. We also searched Embase (Ovid) using an equivalent strategy. Our search strategy is fully specified in appendix A.

We also searched clinical trials registries for trial titles that contained any synonym of 'visual prosthesis' (appendix A). We used the World Health Organization (WHO) International Clinical Trials Registry Platform search portal (https://trialsearch.who.int), which is provided data by several primary clinical trials registries, including the following:

- United States ClinicalTrials.gov- Australian New Zealand Clinical Trials Registry- Chinese Clinical Trial Registry- Japan Primary Registries Network- European Union Clinical Trials Register- German Clinical Trials Register- United Kingdom ISRCTN (International Standard Randomised Controlled Trial Number)

We scanned the resulting trials, looking for references to published articles.

Two authors (LH, SC) independently screened abstracts resulting from MEDLINE, Embase, and WHO International Clinical Trials Registry Platform searches to determine articles of primary interest; we resolved any inconsistencies by discussion and consensus. Two authors (LH, SC) independently read the full- text articles of these publications and extracted data (Data Extraction and Quality Assessment); we resolved any inconsistencies by discussion and consensus. We scanned the reference lists of articles of primary interest for any other publications not discovered by our searches.

### 2.2. Study eligibility criteria

We included interventional or observational studies reporting harms outcomes in participants who had undergone retinal implantation to treat outer- retina degeneration. We included studies of any design, involving any device, including devices approved by regulatory agencies (e.g. the United States Food and Drug Administration), as well as experimental studies of devices yet to be approved to market.

### 2.3. Data extraction and quality assessment

We extracted data from each study using a custom data extraction form (appendix B) that we adapted from a recent systematic review of cochlear implantation [14], and a recent systematic review of retinal implantation efficacy [11]. To guide our assessment of risk of bias, we applied the McMaster Tool for Assessing Quality of Harms Assessment and Reporting [15].

To quantify risk of bias within and across studies, we formed a matrix wherein each column corresponded to a question from the McMaster Tool, and each row corresponded to a clinical trial discovered by our review. We entered a 1 in the matrix for 'yes' answers to McMaster Tool questions, and a 0 for either 'no' answers or 'not reported' answers. We then tallied matrix columns (risk of bias across studies within question) and rows (risk of bias within study across questions). Greater tallies indicated less risk of bias. To guide our assessment of risk of bias, we also referred to the Cochrane Handbook of Systematic Reviews of Interventions, specifically chapters 8 and 25 [16]. To guide our reporting, we used methods described in the PRISMA statement [17].

### 2.4. Meta-analysis of serious adverse events

For each clinical trial discovered by our review, we extracted the time of occurrence of all serious AEs. Using these data, we computed the proportion of cumulative serious AEs versus time in postoperative days, that is, an empirical density of serious AEs. Then, we used Monte Carlo simulation to characterise this density function. This simulation comprised 2000 repeats. On each repeat, for each clinical trial, we randomized the time of occurrence of each serious AE by independently drawing from a uniform distribution of integers ranging from 1 to \(N\) , where \(N\) was the duration of that trial's follow- up in days. For example, if a 12 month clinical trial reported two serious AEs, on each repeat of our Monte Carlo simulation, we randomized the time of occurrence of those events by drawing two integers from a uniform distribution on \([1, 365]\) . On each repeat, after having randomized the time of occurrence of every clinical trial's serious AEs, we pooled times across trials and again computed the proportion of cumulative serious AEs versus time in postoperative days, that is, a simulated cumulative density of serious AEs. The 2000 repeats provided a null distribution of cumulative densities; we compared the empirical density of serious AEs with this null distribution. We repeated this simulation using a log- uniform distribution.

## 3. Results

### 3.1. Description of the included articles

Our MEDLINE and Embase searches returned 563 peer- reviewed journal articles, of which 506 were excluded at the abstract level. We examined the full text of the remaining 57 articles. Our World Health Organization International Clinical Trials Registry search returned 22 peer- reviewed journal articles (most of which concerned efficacy, not safety) not already discovered by our MEDLINE and Embase searches, of which 20 were excluded at the abstract level. We examined the full text of the remaining two articles. The selection process is illustrated in figure 1.

Of the 59 peer- reviewed journal articles selected for full- text review, six articles were excluded because they provided no new data or no useable data. Eleven different devices were discussed in the articles included in our review (table 1). We discovered no studies involving a comparator group (e.g. controlled clinical trials, cohort studies, or case- control studies) that are typically used to calculate harm incidence, relative risks, or odds ratios (i.e. analytic studies). Articles describing clinical trials followed experimental participants for up to 5 years [18]. Amongst the articles describing clinical trials, it was not uncommon to find two articles describing the same trial (e.g. Delyfer and colleagues [19, 20] described outcomes of a single clinical trial at 6 and 24 months postimplantation, respectively). We found articles describing a particular harm to the exclusion of all others (e.g. Peters and colleagues [21] specifically described emotional wellbeing of participants previously reported by Besch et al [22]). We did not apply the McMaster tool (below) to these latter 'single- harm' articles, which closely resembled case- series reports. We found two so- called 'interventional case series' [23, 24], similar in design to the reports of uncontrolled clinical trials discovered by our review. Therefore, we applied the McMaster tool to these case series. In addition to the articles discussed in detail below, our searches discovered several case series reports, and case reports [25- 36].

### 3.2. Risk of bias

We applied the McMaster tool (Methods) to determine the risk of bias in harms assessment and reporting for the clinical trials discovered by our searches as well as two interventional case series. In figure 2 we show the result of the application of the tool; row- tallies (right- most column) assess the risk of bias within a given study; column- tallies (bottom- most row) assess the risk of bias within McMaster question across studies. A greater average indicates a reduced risk of bias. Two authors (LH and SC) independently applied the tool; for only a small minority of questions was discussion required to resolve discrepancy, indicative of good tool reliability. McMaster scores ranged from 3 to 12 which, under the assumption that risk indeed varied from one study to the next, is indicative of good tool validity.

We did not use the McMaster tool to assess the risk of bias of case- series reports (with the exception of two interventional case series) or case reports discovered by our searches. A case report is, per se, at high risk of bias because it provides a detailed description of only one implantee; it follows that all case reports we discovered were at high risk of bias. Nonetheless, we used the adverse events described in case- series reports and case reports to develop a comprehensive list of harms associated with retinal implantation of electronic vision prostheses (appendix C).

### 3.3. Harms associated with implantation

The articles included in our review reported a wide range of harms; commonly reported adverse events, including serious adverse events, were conjunctival erosion and/or dehiscence; retinal detachment, retinal break, and/or retinoschisis; hypotony; and implant displacement. Two other adverse events likely to be underreported, and therefore worthy of discussion, are electrode failure, and vision loss. Many of the articles we discovered did not explicitly provide a clear definition of 'serious adverse event' (figure 2, McMaster question #2), did not report the timing of occurrence of AEs precisely, and did not explicitly state whether all, or simply a subsample of, AEs were reported. Therefore, the determination of the precise number of serious AEs was not always possible. A complete list of adverse events discovered by our review is provided in appendix C; we provide this list to enable future systematic reviews and meta- analyses that use confirmatory search strategies [13]. A list of commonly reported adverse events is provided in table 2.

Few of these articles provided clear definitions of 'serious adverse event', notable exceptions being papers by Humayun and colleagues, and Kitiratschky and colleagues [41, 44] where the standards of reporting were high. However, it seems reasonable to presume that all reported cases of erosion/dehiscence were serious, because all appeared to require surgical intervention, a criterion of seriousness by international standards [60]. Some 20 participants (7.3%) experienced one or more episodes of conjunctival erosion. Conjunctival erosion and/or dehiscence was not specific to any one device; the outcome was also reported in subretinal Alpha IMS and Alpha AMS implantees [44, 48, 49]. For all devices—Argus II, Alpha IMS, Alpha AMS—erosion appeared to be associated with (1) suturing used to anchor the device episcerally, and/or (2) subconjunctival device components straining the conjunctiva.

#### 3.3.2. Hypotony

Ten articles reported hypotony [18, 19, 39, 41, 45, 50, 51, 54, 55, 62]. Few of these articles explicitly provided clear definitions of 'serious adverse event'; some hypotony cases were serious, requiring surgical or medical intervention [60], and others not. A single- arm clinical trial of the Argus II which enrolled 30 participants followed for 3 years reported serious hypotony in 4 participants \((13\%)\) ; hypotony onset for two participants occurred at postoperative months 1 and 4, and onset for two participants occurred between postoperative year one and three [41, 45]. During the first postoperative year, an additional '5- 7' participants \((17\% - 23\%)\) experienced hypotony events judged to be non- serious [41]. At 4.3 years, one participant opted for device explantation due to chronic hypotony and ptosis in the implanted eye [18]. The largest study reporting hypotony was a case- series report [62] on 244 Argus II implant procedures with follow- up duration up to 72 months. Rizzo and colleagues reported, in a post- approval cohort, one or more episodes of severe hypotony in 10 of 186 \((5.4\%)\) implantees at postimplantation year one, and three of 57 \((5.3\%)\) implantees at postoperative year three. A single- arm clinical trial of the Argus II implanted using an adapted surgical technique (superficial temporalis fascia autograft at the sclerotomy site) in 18 participants followed for 2 years reported two cases \((11\%)\) of hypotony following surgery, but no chronic hypotony [19, 20]. Schaffrath and colleagues [51] reported two cases \((4\%)\) of hypotony in a cohort of 47 Argus II implantees followed for up to 12 months.

Hypotony was not specific to any one device. Three single- arm clinical trials of (1) the EPIRET3 device \((n = 6\) ; implantation period \(= 4\) weeks; Roessler et al [39]), (2) the IRIS II device \((n = 10\) ; implantation period \(= 6\) months; Muqit et al [50]), and (3) the IMIE 256 device \((n = 5\) ; implantation period \(= 90\) days; Xu and colleagues [54]) reported one \((17\%)\) ; at postoperative day 1), two \((20\%)\) ; event timing not reported), and one \((20\%)\) ; at postoperative week 6) hypotony events, respectively. Case- series reports, and single- arm clinical trials—both clinical study designs with no comparison group—are not typically used to estimate harm incidence, nor establish causal factors [61]. Regardless, the wide discrepancy between rates of hypotony is difficult to reconcile.

#### 3.3.3. Retinal detachment, retinal break, and/or retinoshisis

Ten articles reported retinal detachment and/or break [18, 41, 44, 45, 49, 51, 55, 63- 65]. A single- arm clinical trial [18, 41, 45] of the Argus II \((n = 30\) participants followed for 5 years) reported three serious retinal detachments (one tractional and two rhegmatogenous) in three participants \((10\%)\) at postoperative month five, month six, and year 4.5; a serious retinal tear in one participant \((3\%)\) during postoperative year one was also reported. A non- serious detachment \((n = 1;3\%)\) , tear \((n = 1;3\%)\) , and retinoshisis \((n = 1;3\%)\) were also reported; in all three cases, occurrence was during postoperative years 2 and 3. A study [51] of the Argus II \((n = 47\) participants followed for up to 12 months) reported serious retinal detachment in three participants \((6\%)\) . Schaffrath and colleagues also reported retinal tear in two participants, and retinoshisis in two participants. However, it is unclear how AEs were determined to be serious or nonserious. Rizzo et al [63] reported a case series of 33 Argus II implantees followed for up to 36 months. The OCT images of 20 patients followed for \(\geq 6\) months were analyzed, revealing that nine patients \((45\%)\) developed retinoshisis. A single- arm clinical trial [55] of the Argus II \((n = 5\) participants followed for 1 year) reported retinal detachment in three participants \((60\%)\) on postoperative day 1, month 1.4, and month 1.9, all occasioning surgical intervention. Two Argus II case studies reported retinoshisis [64, 65].

Retinal detachment, break, and retinosisis were not specific to any one device. A single- arm clinical trial [44] of the Alpha IMS \((n = 9\) implantees followed for 1 year) reported retinal detachment with break occasioning surgical intervention in one participant \((11\%)\) one day after device explantation. Also reported was a retinal break in each of two additional subjects, but in neither case was the break progressive, nor did it occasion intervention. A single- arm clinical trial [49] of the Alpha AMS \((n = 6\) participants followed for up to 12 months) reported retinal detachment occasioning surgery in one participant \((17\%)\) at postoperative month nine.

#### 3.3.4. Implant displacement

Nine articles reported implant displacement [19, 24, 48, 53, 54, 56, 66- 68]. Two studies—a 6 month clinical trial and a case- series report—specifically addressed the event using fundus imaging [19, 66]. Kuehlewein and colleagues analysed images in 32 Alpha IMS or Alpha AMS implantees, with the majority of images revealing array displacement; mean retinal displacement was 0.66 mm (corresponding to a shift in the visual field of \(>2\) degrees). Four participants \((12.5\%)\) required surgical intervention to correct array displacement. A related clinical trial [48] of the Alpha AMS \((n = 15\) participants followed for up to 12 months) reported implant displacement in two participants \((13\%)\) requiring surgical intervention (it is unclear whether these two participants were re- reported by Kuehlewein et al). Delyfer and colleagues analyzed images of 16 Argus II implantees at postoperative months one and six. All images revealed rotation of the array around the tack site (reported mean rotation corresponded to a shift in the visual field of between 1 and 2 degrees). None of Delyfer's observations occasioned surgical intervention and were therefore judged non- serious, but nor were the functional consequences of displacement assessed. A clinical trial [56] of a 44- channel suprachoroidal prosthesis \((n = 4\) ; follow- up \(= 64\) weeks) reported, in one participant \((25\%)\) , anterior displacement of the implant secondary to choroidal effusion, as well as array rotation and temporalward displacement affecting other participants. A case- series report by Arevalo et al [24] \((n = 10\) participants followed for between 4 months and 3.8 years) reported array displacement in one participant \((10\%)\) , and a case- series report by Favardin et al [67] \((n = 4\) participants) reported displacement (intraoperative: 'one patient underwent successful re- tacking right after the first tacking due to inappropriate electrode array position') in one participant \((25\%)\) . A clinical trial [54] of the IMIE 256- channel device \((n = 5\) participants followed for 90 days) reported implant displacement in one participant \((20\%)\) requiring surgical intervention on postoperative day five. A case study [68] of the Argus II reported an intraoperative array displacement over the optic nerve that occasioned surgical correction. A clinical trial [53] of the PRIMA 378- channel subretinal device \((n = 5\) participants followed for 12 months) reported array displacement of approximately 2 mm in one participant \((20\%)\) immediately after implantation. However, it is unclear whether this event occasioned intervention. Few of the studies discovered by our review made measurements specifically targeting implant displacement; it is likely that displacement is an under- reported adverse event, albeit any visual functional consequences of displacement are not yet well understood.

#### 3.3.5. Electrode failure

Electrode failure is likely an under- reported AE; our search revealed few explicit assessments thereof, all involving the 60- electrode Argus II device [24, 64, 69]. The most thoroughgoing data was provided by a case- series report that followed 10 implantees for up to 3.8 years [24]. Testing showed electrode failure in eight of 10 participants \((80\%)\) ; in three participants, failure was attributed to intraoperative electrode damage. Across all 10 participants, the average number of functioning electrodes was 54.8 (range \(= 37 - 60\) ). Two additional case reports following an Argus II implantees reported electrode failure [64, 69]. Seuthe and colleagues followed an implantee for 17 months receiving two implantations: at postoperative month 12, due to implant malfunction, the first device was explanted and a second, replacement device was implanted. At postoperative months three, six, and 12, the number of functioning electrodes were 32, 45, and 42, respectively. At postoperative month 17 (i.e. after implantation of the second device) 59 electrodes were functioning. Hsu and colleagues followed a participant for nine months; impedance testing immediately after implantation revealed 58 functional electrodes.

### 3.4. Meta-analysis of serious adverse events

Our review discovered claims regarding the timing of serious AEs associated with retinal implantation (see Discussion). Despite a lack of evidence for these claims, they nonetheless appear to guide the design, conduct, and reporting of clinical trials. Whether serious AEs tend to occur earlier in follow- up is an open question; here we present the first meta- analysis of harms data aggregated across clinical trials and devices to estimate the distribution in time of serious AEs.

We extracted the time of occurrence of serious AEs from articles reporting clinical trials and interventional case series, and we used this information to estimate the temporal distribution of serious AEs (figure 3). To do so, we relied on the authors' judgement of seriousness. For example, Stanga and colleagues [55] 'recorded seven study- related AEs of which four were classified as SAEs [serious AEs]'. Therefore, four serious AEs are represented in figure 3 associated with that clinical trial (the timing of three of these four events was reported). In three cases, we judged one or more AEs to be serious, despite the authors' not having explicitly done so, because these AEs appeared to conform to the ISO definition for serious AE ('medical or surgical intervention to prevent life- threatening illness or injury, or permanent impairment to a body structure or a body function') [60]. For the clinical trial described by Stingl et al [48], we judged four device explanations due to device malfunction as serious. For the clinical trial described by Fujikado et al [47], we judged a case of iridocyclitis treated with laser iridectomy as serious. For the clinical trial described by Roessler et al [39], we judged a case of hypotony which flattened the anterior chamber and required injection of sodium hyaluronate and revision to sutures, as well as a 'significant inflammation reaction' requiring systemic administration of steroids and antibiotics as serious.

Several clinical trials and interventional case series that appear in the matrix of figure 2 do not appear in figure 3. These studies reported no serious AEs.

We used Monte Carlo simulation to analyze the temporal distribution of serious AEs (Methods). To do so, we used time of occurrence information represented in figure 3. Simulation revealed that the timing of serious AEs is consistent with a log- uniform distribution (figure 4). In lay terms, serious AEs tend to occur early in follow- up; it is reasonable to expect as many serious AEs during postoperative days 1- 10 as during postoperative days 11- 100, or 101- 1000, and so on.

## 4. Discussion

Our systematic review of the peer- reviewed literature shows that retinal implantation of an electrode array to treat outer- retinal degeneration is associated with a wide range of adverse events (AEs) (appendix C). Events common across device designs are conjunctival dehiscence and/or erosion, hypotony, and retinal detachment, break, and/or retinoschisis. Our meta- analysis indicates that serious AEs are log- uniformly distributed throughout follow- up. However, meta- analysis of AEs, serious or otherwise, was not straightforward because the articles we reviewed frequently lacked important details: several articles provided no clear definition of 'serious AE', nor did authors specify whether they were reporting all AEs encountered or a subset thereof. These omissions increase the risk of bias in harms reporting [16]. Efforts to more completely report these details, as well as the reporting of specifics regarding the timing of serious AEs, could ameliorate these shortcomings, and enable future systematic reviews and meta- analyses of retinal implantation.

We discovered claims in the literature regarding the timing of serious AEs associated with retinal implantation, and these claims—despite lacking evidence—appear to guide the design, conduct, and reporting of clinical trials. For example, Xu et al [54] claimed that, because the pre- market clinical trial of the Argus II device reported most serious AEs within 90 days of implantation, that their 90 day trial of the IMIE 256 device provides evidence that it is 'easier and safer to implant' [54]. Whether, generally, serious AEs associated with retinal implantation tend to occur earlier in follow- up is an open question. Here we have presented, for the first time, a meta- analysis of harms data aggregated across clinical trials and devices to estimate the distribution in time of serious AEs. Our analysis revealed that the timing of serious AEs is consistent with a log- uniform distribution. In lay terms, serious AEs tend to occur early in follow- up; it is reasonable to expect as many serious AEs during postoperative days 1–10 as during postoperative days 11–100, or 101–1000, and so on.

Our systematic review found insufficient data to enable rigorous comparison of serious AEs between devices. Despite this lack of data, we discovered claims in the literature regarding the relative safety of different devices, and these claims appear to guide device design [39, 40, 56]. For example, Petoe and colleagues [56] stated that the 'main advantage with a suprachoroidal device is the safety, stability, and ease of the surgical approach... The safety and efficacy of the suprachoroidal approach make it an excellent option to improve functional vision in late- stage RP [retinitis pigmentosa]'. This claim is not new; 10 years earlier, Roessler and colleagues [71] claimed that their epiretinal approach 'allows a complete intraocular implantation with wireless energy and signal supply from outside, while the subretinal prostheses require a trans- choroidal and transscleral cable connection to the energy source outside the eyeball, which may reveal a certain risk potential in this area'. In fact, whether serious AEs are associated more with one approach over another is an open question; to the best of our knowledge, our meta- analysis is the first to comprehensively aggregate harms data across studies and devices to attempt to answer this question—we find the evidence to enable rigorous comparison lacking.

None of the articles we reviewed described studies that involved a comparator group; few articles explicitly stated that the assessment of harms in the unimplanted eye served as control (e.g. Chow et al [38]). The conventional metrics used to report the safety of an intervention, such as harm incidence rates, relative risks, and odds ratios [61], typically require data from analytic studies involving a comparator group (i.e. controlled clinical trials, case- control studies, cohort studies). Studies of medical device safety are rarely placebo- controlled (i.e. no comparator group) because the implantation of a sham device is, arguably, ethically problematic [72]. For the purpose of assessing retinal implant safety, Rizzo and colleagues [62] provided a comparison to another established intraocular device:

Glaucoma drainage device tube exposure has been reported to occur at rates of \(2.5\%\) (144 patients, mean follow- up \(13.4 \pm 0.7\) months) [73], \(6.4\%\) (94 eyes, mean follow- up \(28.9 \pm 20.3\) months) [74], and \(7.1\%\) (85 patients with a minimum of 6 months' follow- up) [75]. Glaucoma drainage devices placed in the inferonasal or inferotemporal quadrants have been reported to cause conjunctival dehiscence in \(57.1\%\) and \(46.2\%\) of eyes [76], respectively. Notably, these adverse event rates are for a relatively well- established therapy, whereas the Argus II by comparison is still early in its development and implementation.

We note, however, that glaucoma drainage devices are sight- saving, whereas retinal implants are not. Therefore, for assessing retinal implant safety, the comparison to glaucoma drainage devices is not straightforward; even if the risks were comparable, the benefits differ considerably.

In addition to glaucoma drainage devices, comparison to other established devices is worthwhile. The House 3 M cochlear implant was the first cochlear implant to receive FDA approval (November, 1984). On lodgement of the pre- market approval application (April, 1984) safety studies showed that five of 163 (3.1%) implantees had experienced 'postoperative complications', and eight of 163 (4.9%) required revision surgery (under local anaesthetic) for device failure [77]. The mean follow- up for this cohort of 163 is somewhat unclear, but it was probably 9 months [77]. A recent 30 year retrospective study of 2311 cochlear implantees found an overall surgical revision rate of 8.3% (i.e. for every 1000 implantees, 83 underwent revision surgery); device failure was the cause of 57.8% of these revisions [78]. The cumulative overall revision rate increased, approximately, linearly by one percentage point per year [78]. There is also precedent for the use of epidemiological data when assessing the safety of a novel intraocular device [79]. Intraocular lenses (IOLs) are now common to treat aphakia following cataract removal. In 1983, IOLs were recommended to the FDA as safe on the basis of favourable comparison to harms outcomes in patients undergoing cataract removal without IOL implantation [80].

This is the first systematic review of harms associated with retinal implantation of an electrode array—an important contribution which nonetheless has limitations as follows. First, we drew harms data from peer- reviewed journals, but we did not search for harms data possibly published elsewhere. Second, the McMaster tool was useful for facilitating the present authors' analysis of how well particular articles identified, analyzed, and reported harms, but it is not clear that the tool generalizes to retinal implants. We believe that the tool provided scores that are good indicators of risk of bias in harms reporting, but readers should exercise caution when interpreting these scores. Third, we considered risk of bias when quantifying the temporal distribution of serious AEs (i.e. we were reluctant to include studies providing no clear definition of 'serious AE', and we excluded case reports), but more data drawn from unbiased safety studies would help to ensure the generalizability of our result. Finally, our review included data pertaining to 11 different devices, but most data was drawn from studies of the Argus II device. More data from non- Argus II devices would help ensure the generalizability of our results.

Systematic review with meta- analysis is the gold standard for evaluating the safety and efficacy of medical interventions. On the basis of the present findings, we recommend that articles describing clinical trials of retinal implantation report a precise definition of 'serious AE' conforming to international standards, specify whether all AEs are reported, and report the precise timing of serious AEs. The adoption of these recommendations would facilitate future meta- analyses which, in turn, would enable the development of a reliable safety profile of the various approaches to retinal implantation of visual prostheses (e.g. subretinal versus suprachoroidal). The development of this profile lacks no urgency; it is needed to inform (1) decision- making by patients and clinicians, (2) study design by clinical researchers, (3) device design by biomedical engineers, and (4) decision- making by regulators, institutional review board (IRB) panelists, and others weighing risks and benefits of implantation. Our recommendations also apply to other emerging approaches to vision loss such as cortical prosthesis [81], as well as stem cell therapies, gene- based therapies, and optogenetics [82].
